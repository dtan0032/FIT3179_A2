Global Suicide Rates (1985 to 2016)

Introduction

According to the World Health Organisation, for every suicide there are many more people who attempt suicide every year. As we will discover, suicide rates have stabalised but it still is relatively high compared to the past. We will be investigating the characteristics of the suicides from 1985 to 2016. These characteristics we will be investigating are range from a combination of countries, sex, age group and Human Development Index.

Global Suicides/100K Population

Here, the line graph shows overall trend in global suicide rates have increased since 1985.

From 1985, it was increasing quickly and peaked in 1995 with a staggering suicide rate of 14660 suicides per 100k population. To put that into perspective, it was that 1 suicide in every 15 person born back in 1985 but increased to 1 suicide for every 7 person born in 1995.

It then slowly decreases to 8254 in 2015 which is roughly 1 suicide in 13 people born.

With the bar chart above, we can see the top 10 countries with the highest suicide rates. The black line that strikes through the bars represent the average suicide rates of the current selection. Using the default “Show All” setting, the global average suicide rate is roughly at 12 suicides per 100,000 population. Lithuania has the highest suicide rate of about 40 suicides per 100,000 population. Consequently, its also the highest in the continent of Europe. As you can see certain parts of the map is greyed out because of our limited dataset that does not cover all countries. Feel free to select different continents to see the top 10 highest suicide rates in each continent.

The scatter plot on the left suggests that as the Human Development Index increases, suicide rate increases as well. According to the United Nations Development Programme, HDI is a summary measure of average achievement in key dimensions of human development: a long and healthy life, being knowledgeable and have a decent standard of living. The HDI is the geometric mean of normalized indices for each of the three dimensions.

As we can see, the age group that contributed the most throughout the years were the people aged above 75 years old. They all have similar increasing trend from 1985 to 1995 then started to decrease down to 2015. The data also suggest that as the age group increases, the number of suicides increases too. We can see this from the lines not intersecting one another at all thought out the years.Therefore, it’s safe to say that as age increases, there are more cases of suicides.

From the collated data from 198 to 2016, we can see given a suicide has occurred, it was 3 times more likely that it was male who committed suicide.

In a study of 4000 patients who engaged in self-harm, men had higher levels of suicidal intent than women (Harriss, 2005). This strongly supports the preposition that men had a higher likelihood of suicidal intent.

A UK Medical Journal also found that general primary care consultation rates were lower in men than women (Wang, 2013). This suggests that men are less likely to know they have mental health conditions and may be a reason why they’re at a greater risk of suicide.
